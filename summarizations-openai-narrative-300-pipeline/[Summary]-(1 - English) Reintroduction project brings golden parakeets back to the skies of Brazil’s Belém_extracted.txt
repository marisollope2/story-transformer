In the heart of Brazil's Belém, the vibrant golden parakeet, coveted for its striking hues, has faced endangerment due to illegal pet trade and habitat loss. The reintroduction project, led by conservationists since 2018, aims to reverse their local extinction. These parakeets not only brighten the forests with their colors but also play a crucial role in seed dispersal for Amazonian flora.

Initially locally extinct in Belém for over a century, the golden parakeets have adapted well to life outside captivity, even reproducing in the wild. The project has successfully released 50 parakeets and plans to introduce an additional 50 in the next two years, hoping to establish self-sustaining populations.

The illegal pet trade, habitat loss, and trafficking pose significant threats to the species. Despite being fairly common in captivity, the golden parakeet population in the wild remains vulnerable. The demand for rare birds as pets fuels a black market, perpetuating the threat to their survival.

Educational efforts and environmental protection are key components of the project. By engaging schools, universities, and the public, awareness about the golden parakeet's significance in the ecosystem is raised. The reintroduction program, located in Utinga State Park, provides a safe space for acclimatization before release, ensuring the parakeets can survive and thrive in their natural habitat.

As the golden parakeets continue to adapt to life in the wild, efforts to expand the project aim to bolster their population naturally. With careful monitoring and community involvement, the hope is to see these magnificent birds flourish once more in the skies of Belém and beyond.
