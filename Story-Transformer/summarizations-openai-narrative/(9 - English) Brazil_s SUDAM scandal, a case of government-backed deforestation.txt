In the late 1990s, Brazil faced the SUDAM scandal, revealing government-backed deforestation schemes in the Amazon. Phantom companies, fraudulent loans, and fake reports supported illegal activities, embezzling millions from the public treasury. Senator-led exploits in agriculture and plantations flourished with the help of falsified projects, funneling funds through intermediary consultancy firms. The scandal implicated powerful figures like Jader Barbalho and Antonio Carlos Magalhães, exposing a complex network of corruption. This revealed just a fraction of the fraudulent activities spanning from 1966 to 2003, totaling over R$4 billion in losses.

Similarly, corruption in the Andean oil sector surfaces with Bolivia's Santos Ramírez Valverde extorting bribes for infrastructure projects. In Ecuador, multinational oil traders were caught diverting millions to secure contracts, and Peru faced scandals involving state-owned firms, bribe payments, environmental damage, and contractual irregularities.

These cases underscore the pervasive nature of corruption within government entities and highlight the detrimental impact on public funds, environmental conservation efforts, and economic stability. The impunity enjoyed by the perpetrators sheds light on the need for stringent anti-corruption measures and oversight to combat such illicit activities.
