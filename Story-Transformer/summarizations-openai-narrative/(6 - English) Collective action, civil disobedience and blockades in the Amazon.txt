The Amazon Basin has a rich history of rebellion and protest, influenced by the resistance of Indigenous people against European and Creole elites. Throughout Bolivia, Peru, and Ecuador, non-violent tactics like highway blockades have been used to protest against inequality. In Brazil, a legacy of unequal land distribution has led to landless peasants occupying absentee landholders' properties.

In Bolivia, civil protests by labor unions and Indigenous communities eventually led to a socialist restructuring of the country. Similarly, Peru witnessed decades of guerilla warfare before reverting to civil protests to address economic injustices and environmental concerns linked to mining.

In Ecuador, Indigenous federations have engaged in national strikes and marches to fight for territorial justice and environmental protection. Despite ongoing civil protests in Amazonian Colombia, they have been overshadowed by the country's history of civil war. The struggles against injustices like stolen elections or authoritarian regimes have continued in Guyana and Suriname.

Overall, the Amazon region has seen a convergence of economic, social, and environmental tensions, prompting various forms of civil disobedience and collective action as a means of challenging entrenched power structures and advocating for justice and rights for marginalized communities across the region.
