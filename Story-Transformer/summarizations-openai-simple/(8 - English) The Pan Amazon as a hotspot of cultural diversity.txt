The Amazon region is culturally rich, with Indigenous nations and diverse migrant groups shaping its cultural tapestry over the past 500 years. Various communities like traditional families highly dependent on forest resources, Indigenous nations retaining their language and heritage in their ancestral lands, riverine communities relying on aquatic resources, and descendants of rubber tappers pursuing livelihoods in forest communities, create a mosaic of social groups in the region.

The Amazon also includes small landholders engaged in family farming practices, cattle producers with large and small-scale operations, corporate entities involved in agribusiness, timber, and extractive sectors, and criminal mafias engaging in illegal activities like drug syndicates, gold mining, and land grabbing.

The state's role, military presence, police challenges, and the power of NGOs, including the Catholic Church, evangelical groups, environmentalists, and social advocates, are essential in influencing and addressing conflicts, environmental concerns, social justice, and governance in the Amazon region. Urban society, including professional elites, entrepreneurs, functionaries, and the proletariat, significantly shape the developments in the region.
