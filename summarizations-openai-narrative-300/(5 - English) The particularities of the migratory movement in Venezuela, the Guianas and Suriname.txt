The text delves into the migratory movement in Venezuela, the Guianas, and Suriname. Initially, it explores Colombia's internal migration history, shaped by civil strife, drug production, and armed guerrilla groups like the FARC. The peace process initiated in 2017 transformed the landscape, replacing guerrilla groups with new criminal entities and sparking a land rush fueled by the drug trade.

In Venezuela and the Guianas, successive governments have exploited the region's natural resources for national development. Migration persists in the gold-rich Bolívar state, overseen by military authorities. Guyana and Suriname heavily rely on mineral resources and aim to exploit their Amazonian territories. This has led to continued migration amidst limited preservation efforts, particularly in Guyana's Essequibo region.

The Guiana Shield, though spared large-scale deforestation, is home to thousands of wildcat gold miners from various countries. In Venezuela, chaotic governance has led to a surge in the population of gold mining areas. While some miners import goods or seek to emigrate, many Venezuelans have sought refuge in makeshift shelters in Brazil.

Throughout the text, the intricate interactions between conflict, development, and natural resource exploitation paint a complex picture of migration in these regions. As political landscapes shift and economic opportunities arise, the movement of populations continues to shape and be shaped by the dynamic environments of the Amazon and its neighboring territories.
