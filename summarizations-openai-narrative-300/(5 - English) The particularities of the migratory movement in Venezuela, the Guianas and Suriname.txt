Internal migration in Colombia, driven by factors like armed conflict, the FARC insurgency, and drug trafficking, has had significant impacts on the country’s landscape and population. The state’s efforts to address land distribution through initiatives like the Caquetá Land Colonization Project were overshadowed by the civil war and illicit activities. The peace process initiated in 2017 led to the dissolution of the FARC but gave rise to new criminal groups, intensifying land acquisition and migration, with regions like Caquetá and Putumayo experiencing shifts in population due to violence and drug cultivation.

In Venezuela and the Guianas, economic exploitation of natural resources has driven migration and territorial disputes. Despite historical efforts by the Venezuelan government to oversee development, mismanagement has led to economic decline. Meanwhile, Guyana and Suriname's focus on mineral resources and lack of conservation areas have influenced migration patterns, especially with the potential development of an international transport corridor. The Guiana Shield, though less impacted by deforestation compared to neighboring regions, attracts wildcat gold miners from various countries.

The stark differences in landscape use and colonial histories among these countries have shaped their approach to development and internal policies. The ongoing challenges with migration, resource exploitation, and territorial disputes underscore the complex social and economic dynamics in these regions.
