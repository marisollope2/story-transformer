In Belém, Brazil, the golden parakeet, known for its striking yellow plumage, faces extinction due to illegal pet trade and habitat loss. Conservationists have initiated a reintroduction project in the city, releasing 50 parakeets back into the wild since 2018. These birds play a vital role in seed dispersal for plants like the açaí berry. They've adapted well, even reproducing in the wild, with plans to release another 50 over the next two years.

The golden parakeet, endemic to the Brazilian Amazon, once locally extinct in Belém for over a century, has been reintroduced with the hopes of creating a self-sustainable population in their native habitat. Poaching and habitat destruction have significantly reduced their numbers, now categorized as vulnerable.

Collaboration between organizations like the Lymington Foundation and IDEFLOR-Bio has enabled the successful breeding and release of these birds, with Utinga State Park acting as a key site for their reintroduction due to its protected natural environment. The project includes educational initiatives to raise awareness about the importance of these birds in the ecosystem.

Despite the successes, challenges remain, especially with previously captive birds. Tracking the released parakeets is challenging, but efforts are ongoing to expand aviaries and release more parakeets into the wild to establish self-sustaining populations across Belém. The ultimate goal is for the golden parakeets to thrive and multiply naturally, ensuring their long-term survival in the Amazon.
