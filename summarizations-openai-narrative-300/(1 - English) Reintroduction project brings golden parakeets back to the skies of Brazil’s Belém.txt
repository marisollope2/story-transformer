In Belém, Brazil, the vibrant yellow and green golden parakeets are being reintroduced into the wild by conservationists to combat their potential extinction due to wildlife trafficking and habitat loss. The birds, valued for their striking colors, play a crucial role in seed dispersal for Amazonian plant species. Over 50 parakeets have been released into the wild since 2018, adapting well and even reproducing, with plans to reintroduce more birds over the next two years.

Once locally extinct in Belém, these parakeets are part of a conservation effort supported by local organizations like IDEFLOR-Bio and the Lymington Foundation. The initiative aims to establish a sustainable population in the area, where the birds had disappeared more than a century ago.

However, challenges persist, especially with illegally captured birds that struggle to adapt to life outside captivity. Despite successful reintroductions, keeping track of the released birds remains a challenge. The project includes environmental education efforts to raise awareness about the importance of these parakeets for the ecosystem in Belém.

The success of the project is evident as released parakeets have been thriving in the wild, consuming native foods, evading predators, and even breeding successfully. With ongoing efforts to expand aviaries and release more birds, conservationists hope to see the golden parakeet population naturally reestablish and spread across Belém and beyond.

Through dedication and collaboration, the vibrant golden parakeets are slowly returning to the skies of Belém, illustrating a promising future for these endangered birds in the Brazilian Amazon.
