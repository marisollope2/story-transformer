The Pan Amazon has a long history of resistance and protest against inequalities and working conditions, with a focus on land distribution issues. Various countries in the region have seen movements using civil disobedience tactics to demand change.

In Bolivia, starting from the 18th century, campesino organizations protested against unequal land distribution through blockades and disruptions. Similarly, the Brazilian Caboclo peasants and Afro-Brazilians rebelled due to exploitation.

Brazil and the Andean Republics witnessed a legacy of Indigenous resistance against domination by elites, leading to uprisings and armed rebellions to fight for their rights and territories. These movements often forced governments to address economic disparities and land distribution issues.

In Peru, Indigenous populations struggled against exploitation, leading to the organization of peasant associations that fought for agrarian reform. The country faced guerilla warfare in the 1980s and 1990s but later saw the rise of organized civil protests, especially regarding land rights and mining impacts.

Ecuador has a history of Indigenous struggles for land and environmental justice, with organizations like CONAIE organizing strikes and marches to demand territorial rights. Blocks on oil production have been successfully advocated for in Ecuador through legal and civil protest strategies.

While Amazonian Colombia has witnessed civil protests, the impact has been overshadowed by decades of civil war. In contrast, land conflicts in Guyana and Suriname have mainly been tied to political events, such as electoral fraud.

Overall, the various movements in the Amazon region have highlighted the persistent struggles for land rights, economic justice, and environmental protection, with civil disobedience serving as a powerful tool for marginalized communities to demand change.
