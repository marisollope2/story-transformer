Indigenous uprisings and resistance to domination have long inspired protests across the Amazon Basin. Non-violent tactics like blocking highways have been used to address land distribution issues in Bolivia, Peru, and Ecuador. In Brazil, unequal land ownership has led to landless peasants occupying land. Through peaceful protests, demands for rights, land, and economic justice have been voiced.

The legacy of resistance in the Amazon spans from European colonization to modern times, with Indigenous people standing up against oppression. In Brazil, the Cabanagem revolt and the establishment of Quilombola communities illustrate this struggle. The unequal distribution of land led to movements like the MST in Brazil advocating for land reform.

Blockades and civil disobedience have been pivotal in Bolivia, with movements spanning from labor unions in the mining industry to demands for land rights. Peru witnessed guerilla warfare before campesino organizations fought for agrarian reform. In Ecuador, Indigenous groups led national strikes for territorial and environmental justice.

In Colombia, civil protests are subdued due to the enduring civil war. However, in Guyana, Suriname, and the Andean Republics, blockades and protests have influenced policy changes. Movements in these regions have fought for economic justice, land rights, and environmental protection through non-violent means, often leading to significant impacts on government actions.
