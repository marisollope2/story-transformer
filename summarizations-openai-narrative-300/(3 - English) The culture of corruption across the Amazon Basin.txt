Corruption is deeply ingrained across the Amazon Basin, with a higher tolerance for fraudulent behavior prevalent in society. The pervasive nature of corruption spans various sectors and jurisdictions, with non-elite corruption more pronounced in certain countries. Elite corruption, on the other hand, is widespread, with wrongdoers enjoying impunity.

While corruption is typically viewed as a governance issue by journalists, it is fundamentally about individual behavior rather than constitutional structures. This unethical behavior extends beyond public institutions to infect private entities and NGOs, leading even typically honest individuals to act unethically due to various pressures like time constraints or perceived necessity.

Living in a culture of distrust and cynicism, citizens are dissatisfied with the status quo but struggle to find a way out of the predicament caused by corruption. Various forms of corrupt practices, such as embezzlement and kickbacks in construction projects, hinder sustainable development and distort investment decisions.

The Corruption Perception Index (CPI), which ranks countries based on corruption levels, sheds light on the prevalence of corrupt behaviors in the Amazon region. Different types of corrupt practices, from bribery to nepotism, affect publicly funded activities across sectors and jurisdictions. While surveys like the CPI attempt to quantify corruption, they may oversimplify the problem, missing nuanced forms of corruption like legal bribes or instances of 'state capture.'

Corruption in the Amazon manifests in various ways, including paying bribes for bureaucratic procedures or using connections for job opportunities. Patronage systems further perpetuate corruption and institutional mediocrity, leading to long-term consequences like inequality. Elite corruption, particularly in the Executive Branch, remains rampant, while the public's low trust in public servants reflects the prevalent impunity for corrupt practices.

Ultimately, corruption in the Amazon Basin presents a multifaceted challenge that hampers development and erodes trust in institutions, illustrating a dire need for systemic change to combat this entrenched issue.
