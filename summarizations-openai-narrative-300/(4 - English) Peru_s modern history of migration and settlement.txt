In the 1970s, Peru undertook a significant transformation with the construction of crucial highways that facilitated migration and settlement in the Amazonian lowlands, particularly attracting individuals from the Andean foothills. Iquitos, a prime destination with over 500,000 residents, experienced rapid growth due to the oil industry. Conversely, the expansion of coca cultivation led to conflicts, displacing roughly 450,000 people from their homes.

The infrastructural developments initiated by a left-wing military government aimed at integrating the Amazonian provinces swiftly expanded during the civilian rule in the 1980s. However, civil unrest hindered progress until the 1990s when under Alberto Fujimori, robust investments in transportation infrastructure were made. This included collaborations with the IDB and establishing the Initiative for the Integration of the Regional Infrastructure of South America to boost strategic planning and public-private partnerships for highway projects.

Migration pathways from the Andean highlands to the Amazon were formed through major and minor highway corridors, shaping the demographic landscape of regions like Ucayali and Huallaga Valley. Settlements burgeoned, notably attracting workers to oil palm plantations and gold mines. The influx of people also affected regions like Madre de Dios, where a gold rush lured miners and shaped socio-economic dynamics.

Moreover, the cultivation of coca, predominantly in the eastern foothills, spurred violent confrontations between insurgent groups and the government, resulting in significant casualties and displacements. Although efforts were made to promote alternative crops in certain areas, the coca trade remains a prevalent issue, impacting various regions differently based on their connectivity to markets.

Peru's complex history of migration, settlement, economic activities, and conflicts underscore the intricate interplay between infrastructural development, resource exploitation, and social challenges in the Amazonian regions.
