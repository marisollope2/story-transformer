In Peru, the construction of key highways in the 1970s led to a significant migration trend from the Andean highlands to the Amazonian lowlands. One major migratory destination is Iquitos, now the largest city in the Western Amazon, driven by the oil industry and service sectors. The cultivation of coca has also shaped Peru's Amazonian regions, leading to violent clashes and displacing thousands of people.

Policies initiated decades ago aimed to connect the Amazonian provinces with the capital, Lima, through highways like the Carretera Federico Basadre. The growth of Peru's Amazonian regions has been fueled by these initiatives, attracting migrants seeking economic opportunities in various sectors.

The Huallaga Valley in particular experienced settlement changes due to highways facilitating access to the region. Coca cultivation, which emerged as a profitable endeavor, influenced the region's development and led to violent conflicts.

The book also delves into significant migration corridors such as the Corredor Interoceánico – Sur and the dynamic influx of miners to Madre de Dios. It points out the impact of coca cultivation in various regions, including clashes between insurgencies and government forces, resulting in significant social upheaval.

Efforts to curb coca cultivation have been partially successful in some regions through multilateral development assistance promoting alternative crops. While some areas have integrated with global markets, those still engaging in coca production remain economically isolated.

The narrative in the book underscores the intricate interplay between migration, infrastructure development, economic activities, and historical events such as the coca trade and armed conflicts, offering a comprehensive look at the modern history of migration and settlement in Peru's Amazonian regions.
