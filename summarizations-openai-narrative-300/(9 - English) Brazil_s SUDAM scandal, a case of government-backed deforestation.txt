In Brazil, the SUDAM scandal of the late 1990s involved a sophisticated corruption scheme that led to the embezzlement of millions of dollars while contributing to deforestation in the Amazon. Phantom companies, fictitious loans, and fraudulent reports were used to support illicit activities related to agricultural ventures in the Amazon Rainforest. Investigations in 1997-1999 uncovered over 150 fake high-value investments, leading to civil lawsuits against businessmen, public officials, and legal entities for compensation.

The scandal revealed the depth of corruption within SUDAM, an agency created to promote development in the Amazon. It involved a complex process where loans and tax credits were diverted to fake businesses and politicians. The scheme included consulting companies run by former agency staff who facilitated project approval and provided false accounting services and invoices. The criminal activity resulted in substantial financial losses, with lawsuits filed against many individuals and entities.

Similarly, in Bolivia, the nationalization of the oil and gas sector led to corruption scandals. Santos Ramírez Valverde extorted a bribe related to a gas-liquid plant construction contract. In Ecuador, international oil trading companies paid bribes to secure contracts with Petroecuador. In Peru, corruption scandals involved state-owned companies, including bribery during bidding processes and allegations of pipeline sabotage.

These scandals underscore the pervasive corruption within the oil and gas industry in the Andean region, with significant financial losses and environmental repercussions. Despite efforts to address these issues, such as liquidating and re-launching agencies like SUDAM, the challenges posed by political corruption and impunity persist, impacting the economy and the environment.
