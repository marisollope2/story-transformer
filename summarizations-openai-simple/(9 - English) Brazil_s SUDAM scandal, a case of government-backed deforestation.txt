In Brazil, the SUDAM scandal involved embezzlement of millions of dollars, fostering deforestation in the Amazon. Corruption schemes included phantom companies, fictitious loans, and fraudulent reports to legitimize illegal operations. Investigations from 1997-1999 revealed over 150 high-value fictitious investments, leading to civil lawsuits against businessmen, officials, and entities for damages to public funds.

The scandal highlighted the audacious corruption where loans were diverted to personal accounts through fake businesses and political influence. Former agency executives' consulting firms facilitated the process by designing fake projects meeting SUDAM’s criteria. The corruption extended to fake reports, accounting fraud, and kickbacks for project approvals. The scandal resulted in lawsuits against individuals and entities, seeking compensation for stolen funds.

The scandal led to the restructuring of SUDAM into ADA. The mastermind, Jader Barbalho, was ordered to repay funds but avoided significant consequences. The scandal illustrates the pervasive political corruption and lack of accountability in Brazil.
