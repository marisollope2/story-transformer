In the second half of the 20th century, major highways incentivized migration in Peru, notably to the Amazonian lowlands from the Andean foothills. Iquitos, a significant destination, attracted immigrants seeking jobs in the oil industry and is now the largest city in the Western Amazon with over 500,000 inhabitants. The cultivation of coca had a profound impact, leading to violent clashes displacing thousands.

Major migration corridors, sponsored by IIRSA, facilitated settlement in the Amazon. Significant growth in regions like Ucayali and Huallaga Valley resulted from these migratory patterns. The expansion of coca cultivation, especially in the upper Huallaga Valley, triggered conflicts, displacements, and impacted economic activities. Efforts to address coca cultivation through alternative crop strategies in regions like Selva Central have shown some success due to development assistance and infrastructure projects.

Overall, the convergence of migration, coca cultivation, and infrastructure development has shaped the modern history of Peru's Amazonian regions, influencing settlement patterns and economic activities.
