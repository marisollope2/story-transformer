The culture of corruption is deeply entrenched in the Amazon Basin, with widespread tolerance for fraudulent behavior. Corruption spans various sectors and jurisdictions, with non-elite corruption more prevalent in some countries like Bolivia, Peru, and Ecuador, while elite corruption is widespread across the region.

Corruption is not just a governance issue but a behavioral problem that extends to public and private entities, including NGOs. Various forms of corruption exist, such as embezzlement and kickback schemes in construction projects, which distort investment decisions and hinder sustainable development.

The Corruption Perception Index (CPI) ranks countries based on corruption levels, showing poor ratings for Amazonian countries. Different types of unethical transactions persist due to long-established permission systems and patronage networks. Patronage systems, prevalent in the region, contribute to institutional mediocrity and reinforce inequalities.

Elite corruption, common in the Executive Branch, enjoys impunity due to a corrupt judiciary. Complex kickback schemes in large infrastructure projects hide malfeasance, leading to wasteful projects with inflated costs and little benefit to the public.
