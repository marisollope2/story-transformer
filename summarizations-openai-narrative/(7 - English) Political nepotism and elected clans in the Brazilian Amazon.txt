Political nepotism and elected clans are prevalent in the Brazilian Amazon, flourishing due to the endemic corruption within governmental institutions. Jader Fontenelle Barbalho personifies political nepotism in Brazil. Starting as governor in 1983, he manipulated his ministerial position for personal gain, amassing land and power. Barbalho was embroiled in various corruption scandals, including embezzlement schemes, which lingered due to slow legal processes.

Another prominent figure, Romero Jucá, abused his positions to benefit from illegal activities. He reduced Indigenous territories, resulting in significant harm, and misused funds meant for Indigenous communities for personal political gain. Both Barbalho and Jucá established political dynasties, with family members occupying key roles in various institutions.

Such instances of political families manipulating systems for self-benefit are not unique to Brazil. The text highlights similar scenarios across Latin America, showcasing the entrenched nature of political nepotism and corruption. These families perpetuate traditional development models and exploit their power for personal gain, epitomizing the challenges posed by unchecked political clans in the Amazon and beyond.
