Corruption is deeply entrenched across the Amazon Basin, showcasing high levels of tolerance for fraudulent activities within society. This phenomenon affects various sectors and jurisdictions, with non-elite corruption prevalent in Bolivia, Peru, and Ecuador. Elite corruption is rampant across countries like Colombia, Brazil, Guyana, and Suriname, where wrongdoers often enjoy impunity. Corruption involves a range of behaviors that subvert public and private entities, impacting governance, natural resource plundering, and infrastructure projects. Despite being viewed as a governance issue, corruption stems from individual behavior, affecting both public and private sectors, as well as NGOs facing financial fraud. Citizens, aware of the culture of dishonesty, often participate in unethical behavior due to system corruption, ultimately eroding trust and fostering a sense of cynicism and dissatisfaction.

The varied forms of corruption in Amazonian societies, from bribes to political patronage, perpetuate dishonest practices and hinder sustainable development. Elite corruption, enduring in all Amazonian nations, contributes to citizen distrust and governmental inefficiency, with the prevalence of impunity exacerbating the situation. The manipulation of funds through kickback schemes in large-scale projects not only perpetuates corruption but also distorts investment decisions, leading to substandard infrastructure and services. The culture of corruption, deeply ingrained in these societies, remains a significant obstacle to progress, while efforts to combat corrupt practices face challenges due to entrenched systems of malfeasance and lack of oversight.
